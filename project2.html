Supply Chain Modeling

This project demonstrated that supply chain-based forecasting using deep learning achieved a higher predictive accuracy for Intel’s stock price than traditional competitor-based models.

The goal of this project is to explore the idea that the systematic study of the movement in prices of businesses that comprise the supply chain of a certain company can lead to predicting the stock's price of this very company. Since a supply chain is "a network between a company and its suppliers to produce and distribute a specific product to the final buyer," we believe we can estimate what is happening at a certain point on a river by observing what is happening upstream and downstream.

If the biggest clients of a certain company are not performing well, it could be expected that their biggest supplier will suffer undesirable consequences. For example, if a key supplier is having trouble procuring a commodity due to climate or political unrest, and that commodity is essential for producing a final product, the impact can ripple through the supply chain. Think, for example, of silicon used in semiconductors for laptops.

We propose building machine learning models where the features are the companies that constitute the supply chain of a chosen target company. This approach offers a unique form of fundamental analysis centered around the supply chain to forecast stock prices using machine learning.

To test the deep learning model, Intel Corp was selected as the target. The features were as follows:

  • Suppliers: Applied Materials, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing
  • Distributors: Arrow Corporation, Avnet Inc, Synnex Corporation
  • Clients: Dell, HP, Lenovo

We also built a comparative model using the stock prices of Intel’s direct competitors: AMD, NVIDIA, Qualcomm, Broadcom, Micron Technologies, and Texas Instruments. We ran deep learning and machine learning regression models for both analyses to compare forecasting performance.

The deep learning model was built in Keras with hyperparameters optimized through trial and error. We used k-fold cross-validation to ensure consistency across samples.

Performance was tracked using mean absolute error across epochs, and a loss comparison between training and validation sets to assess underfitting or overfitting.

The prediction results clearly show that the supply chain-based model outperforms the competitor-based approach. The scatter plots of predicted vs. actual values show tighter clustering and better fit lines in the supply chain model.

Correlation between predicted and actual prices:

  • Supply Chain (Deep Learning): 0.9872
  • Supply Chain (Regression): 0.8614
  • Competitors (Deep Learning): 0.9602
  • Competitors (Regression): 0.8277
